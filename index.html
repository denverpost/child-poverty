“I should run on the double. I think I’m in trouble.” — Lindsey Buckingham, in Courtroom 3H

George Brady has a thing for weed.

It relaxes his mind, he says, so he smokes it even though the judge, his caseworker and his therapist keep telling him it’s messing with his medication for schizoaffective disorder. He thinks it gives him clarity, but to everyone else, George seems more confused and forgetful when he is high.

“Stay away from the marijuana,” Judge Barajas is telling him for the 20th time at least. “I know it’s a struggle for you, and especially the spice. Are we on the same page?”

“Yeah.”

George, 28, sees the world through an altered reality, a haze so thick it is improved but not lifted by his monthly medication shot. He hasn’t hurt anyone but has been to jail 36 times, usually for what he terms misunderstandings. His most recent arrest was for loitering too long at a truckstop Wendy’s. Then he was stripped of his bus pass last summer because an agent caught him trying to sell — George says give — a bus voucher to another rider.

He continued to ride anyway, because how else would he get from the motel in the shadow of Interstate 70 east of Denver to his court-ordered therapy appointments downtown?

It happens all the time, this total disconnect between two systems. The courts are paying for George to get therapy and stay out of jail, while the city transportation system is taking away the bus pass he uses to get to therapy.

George did not realize as it was happening that he was losing his bus pass; he figures it out in court several days later. He is wide-eyed, eyebrows raised, head cocked, giving the judge one of his signature dumbfounded looks. “RTD is everywhere! That means I can’t be nowhere!” he says.

George is tall and softspoken. He wears his pants baggy, still with the store tags, and lets his hair grow until it is sticking up. He says he doesn’t know what schizoaffective disorder — a combination of schizophrenia and mood disorder that alters his perception of reality — is, really. What he has, he says, is “hard times.”

“It’s not like I hear imaginary voices or see imaginary people,” he says, sitting on his motel room bed, snacking on sunflower seeds and chuckling. “I’ve been feeling a lot better. I’ve been getting rest. I’ve been eating. I’ve been exercising, playing basketball. I feel a lot better.”

He’s taking Trazadone to help him sleep and a monthly shot of Invega “to be clearing my thoughts,” George said.

“It doesn’t work.”

Darcy has been seeing a psychiatrist. Within a few weeks on antidepressants, she is noticeably changed: less irritable, more social, more optimistic about finding her own apartment.

She never wants to return to jail. She says she is done with booze. She says she is done with her ex, also an alcoholic. Until the day she runs into him as he is being released from jail and she is heading into Thursday court.
