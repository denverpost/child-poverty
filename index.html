Below the Line Childhood poverty in Colorado in 2oo8

The number of children under the age of 18 who live below the poverty line in the United States increased by 9% between 2000 and 2006. In Colorado, the number of children living in poverty grew by 73%, the highest rate increase in the nation. The Denver Post has compiled a multimedia package that details in, pictures and video the stories of some of those children.

Broomfield, Colorado

Living on the edge without Mom

Elizabeth “Missy” Hessling, 8, works with paraprofessional Cyndi Dumont during reading class at Birch Elementary School in Broomfield.

Elizabeth “Missy” Hessling, 8, sweeps the basement of her father's parents. Her grandparents’ basement has become the play area for her and her two brothers every other Saturday while their dad, Roy, works.

Roy Hessling’s world crashed down around him in January 2007, when his wife, Jill, a working mom, died at 29 after heart surgery. He now raises their three children, ages 8 to 11, in the suburbs while working as a mechanic at a Brakes Plus shop. The children go to his in-laws’ home after school and wait for their father to pick them up after work. They usually stay at his in-laws' house and eat dinner as a family. “ e’re just basic people trying to get by, ” he says.

The Dorsey Family

Denver, Colorado

Seeking shelter in Sun Valley

Photo story by Craig F. Walker

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, lies under his bed. He said that’s where he goes when he’s in trouble. Shawnwunz’a was home from school after being given a three—day suspension for having hit another student. He was spending the majority of the day in his room. Earlier he ventured into the living room and went unnoticed for a few minutes. But then his mother yelled, “I told you to get out of my living room.” He bowed his head and returned to his room, where he retreated under the bed and banged on the metal frame. Dorsey, who could hear the noise, said, “He’s mad and pouting.” She then shouted to her son, “I know you mad, but that’s too bad.”

Shawnette Dorsey, 32, sits on her bed ironing clothes for her three children. She’s up early every day to make sure they get ready for school on time. She takes pride in her family’s appearance. “I just want my kids to be dressed ’cause that’s all I can do,” she said.

Eight—year—old Shawnwunz’a Smith is slow to get ready for school. Staring at the television in his room, he was more interested in a cartoon than in putting on his clothes.

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, strides over the snow on his way to Fairview Elementary School. The second-grader, who was 15 minutes late, was worried that he would miss the free breakfast offered at the school.

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, chews on a finger while listening to a lecture from Fairview Elementary School principal Norma Giron. Instead of recess that day, the school's second-graders were sent to the library to talk about their bad behavior the previous day: lining up improperly, tackling during flag football and talking back to teachers.

Eight-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith tries to keep schoolmates in line, literally, after lunch at Fairview Elementary School. Principal Norma Giron said that despite Shawnwunz’a’s behaviorial issues, he is intelligent. “(But he) never learned any kind of boundaries,” she said. “He’s verbally aggressive and inappropriate. Academically, he could do the work. That shouldn’t be an issue, because he has the intelligence to do it.”

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, watches Analisa Angel serve macaroni and cheese to the children enrolled in the day-care program at Sun Valley Youth Center. The center offers tutoring, mentoring, meals and recreation to neighborhood children who have few alternatives.

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, sits quietly during a short bus ride home from Sun Valley Youth Center's after-school program at Fairview Elementary School. His mother didn't pick him up that day, so he needed to take the bus instead of walking the two blocks home.

Shawnette Dorsey, 32, helps her son Shawnwunz’a Smith with his homework in the living room of their apartment. Shawnwunz’a, 8, wasn’t at school because he had been suspended for three days for hitting another student. So he's in trouble at home as well.

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, stands for 5 minutes in the corner he was sent to by his mother, Shawnette Dorsey. The punishment was handed down because he threw a tantrum after being told to put his 13-month-old half sister down. “I want Shawnwunz’a to stop having tantrums,” the 32-year-old mother of three said. “That irritates me to death. And he do it everywhere. If he wants something, I say, ’I&rlquo;m not giving it to you,’ (and) he has a tantrum and hollers out loud. I don’t like it at all.”

Thirteen-month-old Shalisiana Fenwick watches her mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, unpack a box of food donated by her church. Dorsey said she primarily uses food stamps to feed her family. “I ain’t used to like food stamps, but I use them now,” she said. ”I used to be shy at one time, but I ain’t shy no more.”

Shawnwunz‘a Smith, 8, surveys the contents of his family‘s refrigerator. He‘s hungry but knows he‘ll have to wait until dinner. Still, he‘s allowed a glass of apple juice. His mother was making fried chicken and french fries. The boy said his mother is a great cook and he likes everything she makes.

Before Thanksgiving dinner, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, takes the hand of her son Shawnwunz‘a Smith, 8, who takes the hand of his half brother, Shonquez Dorsey, 13, who takes the hand of his half sister, Shalisiana Fenwick, 13 months. Dorsey leads the family in the saying of grace. ‘Most Heavenly Father, you are the Lord, our God. Thank you for the food that we have.“

Shalisiana Fenwick, 13 months, sits quietly with her eyes closed as her mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, does her hair before church. Dorsey was singing along to gospel music on TV. The song was fast and upbeat, repeating: ”I’ll get by with what I got, I‘ll get by with what I got, I‘ll get by with what I got.“ Dorsey added, ”Amen to that.“

After picking up paperwork regarding son Shawnwunz&rlquo;a Smith‘s three-day suspension from Fairview Elementary School, a frustrated Shawnette Dorsey tries to maneuver the stroller carrying her daughter down a couple stairs. Although the 8-year-old boy was trying to help, Dorsey would rather have taken care of it herself. ”I‘m independent, so I take care of my kids myself,“ she said. ”I‘m the serious type. I ain‘t the playin‘ type.“

Arriving at the Family Dollar store, Shawnwunz‘a Smith, 8, heads straight for the toys. After playing with a guitar, a ball and a music machine, he got his hands on a toy car and went searching for his mother, who was in the food section. “Can I get this?” he asked. He got quick and simple responses: “No” and “Put it back.” He began to make a fuss but thought better of it and walked away.

Shawnette Dorsey, 32, and Eddie Kelley perform a duet while singing with the choir at the Kingdom of Glory Christian Center, a former hardware store on Welton Street in Denver. Dorsey’s faith in God runs deep, with the seeds planted during her childhood in a strict Baptist family in Louisiana. “I go to church on Sunday, I have choir practice on Saturday,” she said. “I go to church every Sunday and I sing my praise to him.”

Eight-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith, center, joins in prayer during Sunday school at the Kingdom of Glory Christian Center. His mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, said she often turns to the Lord when challenged as a mother. “I pray,” she said. “I say, ‘Lord, I’m giving my kids to you.’”

Back home from school, Shonquez Dorsey, 13, is quick to joyfully greet his half brother, 8-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith. The embrace turned into a playful wrestling match. Shonquez said he likes hugging his family. “I might not see them again. So I hug them everyday,” he said. ”Anything can happen.“

Shawnette Dorsey and her children excitedly decorate their Christmas tree. Thirteen-month-old Shalisiana Fenwick and Shonquez Dorsey, 13, watch their mother help Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, place the star atop the tree. The family lives on unemployment, food stamps and Medicaid. ”It’s enough to do what I gotta do, enough that I do for paying my bills,“ Dorsey said. ”I maintain. You know what I’m sayin’? If I ain’t got it, I ain’t got it. If I got it, I got it.“

With the glow of downtown Denver providing the backdrop, 8-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith follows his mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, left, and Lestina Anderson as they roll their babies home from the Family Dollar store on Federal Boulevard.

Denver’s Sun Valley, the poorest neighborhood in Colorado, is a welcome shelter for Shawnette Dorsey, 32, and her three children, ages 13 months, 8 and 13. The family, who had bounced from apartment to shelter to friends’ homes, finally landed an apartment at Decatur Place for single parents willing to enter a two-year program to get back on their feet. She and the kids say grace and go to church on Sunday. Still, life isn’t easy. Both of her sons have been in and out of trouble at school. In September, she lost her job, so now the family lives off unemployment checks, food stamps and Medicaid.

The Martinez Family

Capulin, Colorado

Poverty for the ages in Capulin

Photo story by Judy Dehaas

Jozif Martinez, 4, drinks Kool-Aid at the dining room table in his family’s home in Capulin. He had tortillas and ham for dinner in the bowl.

Michael Martinez, 3, plays in the yard at his family’s home in Capulin. Visible in the background is the home belonging to his uncle Frank Martinez.

Eli Martinez Jr. loads a wheelbarrow with wood that his family will burn in their two iron stoves which heat their house in Capulin.

Three-year-old Michael Martinez climbs a ladder set against the 80-year-old adobe part of his family’s home in Capulin.

Michael Martinez, 3, reaches for the hand of his cousin Kayla Martinez on the slide that sits on the land that their families share in Capulin.

Eli Martinez Jr., 11, looks through boxes of food delivered to his family by RoAnne Gomez, the Conejos County family advocate for La Gente, a nonprofit agency that assists needy families in San Luis Valley. The food was provided by a donor that adopted the Martinez family for Christmas.

Jasmine Martinez, 14, applies lip gloss in her bedroom before catching the bus for Centauri Middle School.

Eli Martinez, 39, holds his 6-month-old son, Adrian, while another son, 3-year-old Michael, watches TV at their home in Capulin. Martinez stays home with his three youngest children while his wife, Sherry, works at a hospital in La Jara.

Joaquine Martinez, 10, visits with a horse and a couple of ponies on a rural road outside his hometown of Capulin.

Joaquine Martinez, 10, walks through an old cemetery outside his hometown of Capulin. He enjoys looking at the old headstones and markers of the graves, which date to the 19th century.

Three-year-old Michael Martinez, 3, and brother Joaquine, 10, walk down an unpaved road in Capulin, located in Conejos County.

Joaquine Martinez, 10, scans an advertising flier for specials at Jack’s Super Market in La Jara, while brother Eli Jr., 11, compares prices for bacon.

Three-year-old Michael Martinez watches, with great anticipation, his sister Heather make tortillas in the dining room. The 16-year-old makes them daily for the family.

Heather Martinez, 16, prepares homemade tortillas for her family in the kitchen as 3-year-old Michael watches. She makes the tortillas every day.

Sherry Martinez reads to her sons Michael, 3, left, and Jozif, 4, in their living room.

The Martinez family and other members of the congregation at St. Joseph’s Catholic Church hold hands during Mass.

William Martinez, 24, lifts his sister Heather, 16, outside their family’s home in Capulin. William, who hurt his shoulder installing telephone poles while living and working on the road, recently returned home to get his shoulder healthy again and to spend time with his family.

Joaquine Martinez, 10, sleeps in the bed he shares with his 11-year-old brother, Eli Jr. Their adobe home, located in Capulin, was built by the boys’ grandfather, Senon, more than 75 years ago.

Sherry and Eli Martinez, ages 40 and 39, are raising eight of their nine children in an adobe home and an adjacent building on their property. The adobe house was built by Eli's father more than 75 years ago. Sherry earns $21,000 a year at a hospital nine miles away, but Eli, a war veteran, is unable to hold a job. Poverty has been a way of life for generations in this part of the San Luis Valley. But Eli is too proud to accept food stamps. Instead, he spends time with the children, hunts for meat and takes seasonal jobs in nearby potato farms. All the kids pitch in to fix meals, feed the animals and gather firewood for the stoves.

The Atencio Family

Grand Junction, Colorado

An army of poor in Grand Junction

Photo story by Reza Marvashti

Nevaeh Atencio, 5, plays on the living room of the family home in Grand Junction. With 27 people residing on the property, the house’s living room doubles as a sleeping area.

Alicia Atencio, 33, sorts through clothing for her seven children as her husband, Orlando, 26, looks on. Not having a room of its own in the house, her family keeps its belongings in book bags, luggage and anything else they can use to separate belongings among the seven children..

Nia Atencio looks to her parents after a stay at the “timeout wall” in the family home. Strict discipline is administered in the home of 27 people, so at times the wall is lined with children of various ages serving their punishment.

Lionel Atencio removes his jacket before heading inside the family home for dinner. As the sun goes down, the monumental task of cooking for 27 people pops up.

From left, Jamarion Atencio, Fai’zon Mitchell, Nia Atencio, Santiana Atencio and Nevaeh Atencio wake from their afternoon naps on the living room floor of the family home. The living room serves as the sitting room during the day and a bedroom at night.

Alicia Atencio, 33, does daughter Nevaeh’s hair every morning before school. Atencio, who was a stylist in New Mexico, hopes to get her license in Colorado. Monetary concerns, however, have slowed her efforts.

Nevaeh Atencio looks toward her teacher during writing exercises at Pear Park Elementary School in Grand Junction.

Nevaeh Atencio and her brother Kyeran Daniels share a smile during breakfast at Pear Park Elementary before school begins. Every morning, they share time together over school breakfast.

Five-year-old Nevaeh Atencio goes over her kindergarten spelling homework with her mother, Alicia, while Orlando Jr. naps in his swing. Juggling housework, homework and motherly duties, Alicia rarely has a moment to herself. “Not having a space of our own is the hardest part,” she said of her family’s situation.

Orlando Atencio, 26, holds his daughter Santiana, 2, outside the family home in Grand Junction. The dedicated father and husband, who was laid off, spends most of his time at home with his seven children.

Lionel Atencio scrubs his hands before heading to school in the only bathroom of the home. With 27 people on the property, a quiet moment in the bathroom is rare. All family members are told to respect the privacy of others.

On his way to school, Kyeran Daniels turns around to see how his three siblings are doing as they make their way across the snowy roadway in Grand Junction. They make the short walk to their elementary school daily.

Nevaeh Atencio runs up the long driveway to her home after half-day kindergarten at Pear Park Elementary School in Grand Junction. The Atencio family’s rental home is tucked into a small parcel of land.

Ron and Vickie Atencio’s modest Grand Junction house and an adjacent RV are home to 27 people, including 13 children. The Atencios’ three sons — victims of layoffs in the energy industry — and their families comprise most of the household. The living room transforms into a bedroom at night for many of the children, and fixing meals is an adventure. Alicia Atencio, the wife of one of the sons, said it’s like cooking for an army.
