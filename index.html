Below the Line Childhood poverty in Colorado in 2oo8

The number of children under the age of 18 who live below the poverty line in the United States increased by 9% between 2000 and 2006. In Colorado, the number of children living in poverty grew by 73%, the highest rate increase in the nation. The Denver Post has compiled a multimedia package that details in, pictures and video the stories of some of those children.

The Dorsey Family

Denver, Colorado

Seeking shelter in Sun Valley

Photo story by Craig F. Walker

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, lies under his bed. He said that’s where he goes when he’s in trouble. Shawnwunz’a was home from school after being given a three—day suspension for having hit another student. He was spending the majority of the day in his room. Earlier he ventured into the living room and went unnoticed for a few minutes. But then his mother yelled, “I told you to get out of my living room.” He bowed his head and returned to his room, where he retreated under the bed and banged on the metal frame. Dorsey, who could hear the noise, said, “He’s mad and pouting.” She then shouted to her son, “I know you mad, but that’s too bad.”

Shawnette Dorsey, 32, sits on her bed ironing clothes for her three children. She’s up early every day to make sure they get ready for school on time. She takes pride in her family’s appearance. “I just want my kids to be dressed ’cause that’s all I can do,” she said.

Eight—year—old Shawnwunz’a Smith is slow to get ready for school. Staring at the television in his room, he was more interested in a cartoon than in putting on his clothes.

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, strides over the snow on his way to Fairview Elementary School. The second-grader, who was 15 minutes late, was worried that he would miss the free breakfast offered at the school.

Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, chews on a finger while listening to a lecture from Fairview Elementary School principal Norma Giron. Instead of recess that day, the school's second-graders were sent to the library to talk about their bad behavior the previous day: lining up improperly, tackling during flag football and talking back to teachers.

Eight-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith tries to keep schoolmates in line, literally, after lunch at Fairview Elementary School. Principal Norma Giron said that despite Shawnwunz’a’s behaviorial issues, he is intelligent. “(But he) never learned any kind of boundaries,” she said. “He’s verbally aggressive and inappropriate. Academically, he could do the work. That shouldn’t be an issue, because he has the intelligence to do it.”

The punishment was handed down because he threw a tantrum after being told to put his 13-month-old half sister down. “I want Shawnwunz’a to stop having tantrums,” the 32-year-old mother of three said. “That irritates me to death. And he do it everywhere. If he wants something, I say, ’I&rlquo;m not giving it to you,’ (and) he has a tantrum and hollers out loud. I don’t like it at all.”

Thirteen-month-old Shalisiana Fenwick watches her mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, unpack a box of food donated by her church. Dorsey said she primarily uses food stamps to feed her family. “I ain’t used to like food stamps, but I use them now,” she said. ”I used to be shy at one time, but I ain’t shy no more.”

Shawnwunz‘a Smith, 8, surveys the contents of his family‘s refrigerator. He‘s hungry but knows he‘ll have to wait until dinner. Still, he‘s allowed a glass of apple juice. His mother was making fried chicken and french fries. The boy said his mother is a great cook and he likes everything she makes.

Before Thanksgiving dinner, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, takes the hand of her son Shawnwunz‘a Smith, 8, who takes the hand of his half brother, Shonquez Dorsey, 13, who takes the hand of his half sister, Shalisiana Fenwick, 13 months. Shalisiana Fenwick, 13 months, sits quietly with her eyes closed as her mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, does her hair before church. Dorsey was singing along to gospel music on TV. The song was fast and upbeat, repeating: ”I’ll get by with what I got, I‘ll get by with what I got, I‘ll get by with what I got.“ Dorsey added, ”Amen to that.“

After picking up paperwork regarding son Shawnwunz&rlquo;a Smith‘s three-day suspension from Fairview Elementary School, a frustrated Shawnette Dorsey tries to maneuver the stroller carrying her daughter down a couple stairs. Although the 8-year-old boy was trying to help, Dorsey would rather have taken care of it herself. ”I‘m independent, so I take care of my kids myself,“ she said. ”I‘m the serious type. I ain‘t the playin‘ type.“

Arriving at the Family Dollar store, Shawnwunz‘a Smith, 8, heads straight for the toys. After playing with a guitar, a ball and a music machine, he got his hands on a toy car and went searching for his mother, who was in the food section. “Can I get this?” he asked. He got quick and simple responses: “No” and “Put it back.” He began to make a fuss but thought better of it and walked away.

Shawnette Dorsey, 32, and Eddie Kelley perform a duet while singing with the choir at the Kingdom of Glory Christian Center, a former hardware store on Welton Street in Denver. Dorsey’s faith in God runs deep, with the seeds planted during her childhood in a strict Baptist family in Louisiana. “I go to church on Sunday, I have choir practice on Saturday,” she said. “I go to church every Sunday and I sing my praise to him.”

Eight-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith, center, joins in prayer during Sunday school at the Kingdom of Glory Christian Center. His mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, said she often turns to the Lord when challenged as a mother. “I pray,” she said. “I say, ‘Lord, I’m giving my kids to you.’”

Back home from school, Shonquez Dorsey, 13, is quick to joyfully greet his half brother, 8-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith. The embrace turned into a playful wrestling match. Shonquez said he likes hugging his family. “I might not see them again. So I hug them everyday,” he said. ”Anything can happen.“

Shawnette Dorsey and her children excitedly decorate their Christmas tree. Thirteen-month-old Shalisiana Fenwick and Shonquez Dorsey, 13, watch their mother help Shawnwunz’a Smith, 8, place the star atop the tree. If I ain’t got it, I ain’t got it. If I got it, I got it.“

With the glow of downtown Denver providing the backdrop, 8-year-old Shawnwunz’a Smith follows his mother, Shawnette Dorsey, 32, left, and Lestina Anderson as they roll their babies home from the Family Dollar store on Federal Boulevard.

Denver’s Sun Valley, the poorest neighborhood in Colorado, is a welcome shelter for Shawnette Dorsey, 32, and her three children, ages 13 months, 8 and 13. The family, who had bounced from apartment to shelter to friends’ homes, finally landed an apartment at Decatur Place for single parents willing to enter a two-year program to get back on their feet. She and the kids say grace and go to church on Sunday. Still, life isn’t easy. Both of her sons have been in and out of trouble at school. In September, she lost her job, so now the family lives off unemployment checks, food stamps and Medicaid.

An army of poor in Grand Junction

Eight people, including three children, live in Rodney and Kalin Cook’s two trailers, which are boarded together with plywood near the tiny town of Hooper in Saguache County, the poorest in the state. She lives with her unemployed mother, and the household survives on welfare checks. Razey, who has come out that she is gay and whose girlfriend now lives with the family, is taking college classes. She hopes to get a degree in social work.

Patricia Guernsey, 35, lives with her three children and 54-year-old mother in an apartment at the Kings Inn Motel on East Colfax Avenue. They live off unemployment checks, food stamps and disability. Linda Starkey, their grandmother, takes 5-year-old Tatiana to school on an RTD bus. The family, which is often behind on its rent, continuously faces eviction.

Photographers

Craig F. Walker, a Denver Post veteran for twelve years, far left, has tackled a variety of assignments during his career, such as the events following the Sept. 11 terrorist attacks, the war in Afghanistan, the inauguration of Afghan President Hamid Karzai and multiple deployments of American troops in Kuwait and Iraq. Joe Amon, a former Marine, received his training in photography from the The Art Institute of Pittsburgh after being laid off from the steel mill in his hometown, Little Washington, Pennsylvania. While working at Ft. Lauderdale’s Sun Sentinel, he won the Robert F. Kennedy Award for International Photography for his project on aids orphans. R. J. Sangosti, a Colorado native, brings a sensitive and inventive approach to his subject matter and continues to engage Post readers with both his daily and long-term assignments. Judy DeHaas, was awarded the Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting with a team of Dallas Morning News journalists for their global project on violent human rights abuses against women. DeHaas joined the photography staff at The Denver Post immediately after the closure of The Rocky Mountain News. Reza Marvashti, formerly a roustabout for the Big Apple Circus, joined the staff at the Free Lance-Star in Fredericksburg, Virginia, in 1998 as a photojournalist, winning many awards, including the Atlanta Photojournalism Conference Photographer of the Year. He was hired as a photo editor at the Denver Post in 2008. Andy Cross, has covered a wide variety of assignments during his twelve years at the Post, including national and international news and sports. He was among the first western journalists to arrive in Iraq during Operation Iraqi Freedom. Hyoung Chang, a Japanese-born Korean, has traveled extensively in the Middle East during his twelve years at the Post, covering U.S. military actions in Afghanistan, Pakistan, Jordan, Lebanon, Kuwait, Turkey, Iraq and Egypt, as well as social issues in the United States.
